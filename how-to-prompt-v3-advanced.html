The four advanced moves
5
Ask It to Push Back
The move most people never think to make
The key insight: AI models are trained to be helpful and agreeable. Left to their own devices, they'll validate your plan. You have to explicitly ask for disagreement — the model won't volunteer it. The pre-mortem forces a specific story about failure, not a risk list. That story is almost always more useful. One round of forced dissent before you ship is worth more than three rounds of revision after.
Steel-man critique
What's wrong with this plan? Steel-man the strongest objections. Don't hedge — I want the real critique.
Pre-mortem — use before you ship
Imagine it's six months from now and this failed. Write the post-mortem. What went wrong, and why?
6
Ask It to Interview You First
Flip the relationship — let it surface the gaps you didn't know you had
The key insight: When you write prompts cold, you under-specify. The model fills gaps with averages. The output is fine-but-generic. Flip it — ask the model what it needs to know first. This reverses the expected relationship. You came with a task; now it's asking you questions. That reversal is exactly the point.
Template — interview mode
Before you write this, ask me the five most important questions you'd need answered to do this well. Wait for my answers before producing anything.
When to use it: Any nontrivial task — a document, a strategic question, a complex piece of content. Not for quick lookups.
7
Decompose Big Asks Into Chains
One prompt, one job
The key insight: Most people write kitchen-sink prompts. The model quietly drops two jobs, or satisfies all four shallowly. A mega-prompt gives you bad output with no indication of where it went wrong. Think of prompts like functions — when something breaks in a chain, you find exactly which step failed. If your prompt has more than 2–3 distinct responsibilities, break it up.
Instead of this
"Summarize this document, identify the three key themes, rewrite it for a non-expert audience, and format everything as a table."
→
Chain these
1
Summarize this document in five bullets.
2
Based on that summary, what are the three key themes? One sentence each.
3
Rewrite bullet 2 for someone with no background in this topic. Plain language, no jargon.
8
Ask It to Rewrite Your Prompt
When a prompt has frustrated you 2–3 times — hand it back first
The key insight: We write prompts in our own head-voice, with assumptions baked in that the model doesn't share. When you hand the prompt back, it adds specificity you didn't think to include, tightens the task statement, and surfaces structural problems. Both Anthropic and OpenAI ship dedicated prompt-generator tools because this pattern is that reliable.
Template — hand it back
Here's my prompt: [paste your prompt]. Rewrite it to get a better result from you. Tell me what you changed and why.

Try It
Take something you're about to send, decide, or build. Run it through one of the advanced moves before you ship it.
  • 1Pick something real — a plan, a doc, a message you're about to finalize.
  • 2If it's a plan or decision: run the pre-mortem prompt. See what story it tells about failure.
  • 3If it's a document you haven't started: use "interview first" before you write anything.
  • 4If a prompt has been frustrating you: hand it back and ask for a rewrite. Notice what it changes.
