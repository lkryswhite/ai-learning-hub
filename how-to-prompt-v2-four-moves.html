Four Moves That Change
Your Output.

Audience Spec · Show Examples · Ask for Options · Critique Loop

Use this after watching Video 2. Each move takes ~30 seconds to apply. The compound effect is real.

The four moves
1
Get Specific About the Audience
The same task for different audiences should feel like different documents
The key insight: "A student" is an audience. "A pre-med junior" is better. "A pre-med junior two weeks from their exam who's overwhelmed and skimming" actually reshapes the output. This applies beyond content — emails, strategy docs, Slack messages.
Upgrade your audience spec
Not
"Write for students."
Better
"Write for pre-med juniors."
Best
"Write for a pre-med junior two weeks from their exam who understands basic biology but finds biochemistry intimidating. They're studying in 20-minute windows and will skim anything that doesn't feel immediately relevant."
2
Show It What Good Looks Like
Don't describe your voice. Paste it.
The key insight: Two or three examples consistently outperform 500 words of instructions. The model mimics what it sees far more reliably than what you say. When examples and instructions conflict, the model follows the examples — they're the loudest signal in the prompt.
Template — paste your examples
Here are two examples of the style I'm going for:

[paste example 1]

[paste example 2]

Write [your thing] in the same voice. Match the tone, sentence length, and level of formality.
This also works for format — paste a filled-in row and say "follow this schema." Demonstrations beat descriptions.
3
Ask for Options Before You Commit
"Distinctly different" is the load-bearing phrase
The key insight: Most people use AI as an executor — one output, then edit. That means you're always working inside the first interpretation the model landed on. Without "distinctly different," you get five near-duplicates. With it, you get real strategic range.
Template — ask for range
Give me five distinctly different approaches to this. They should differ in structure, framing, and core assumptions — not just surface phrasing. Don't hedge between them.
Best for: content strategy, communications, framing a difficult message, or any task where you're not sure yet what "good" looks like.
4
Ask It to Critique Its Own Output
The most underused habit
The key insight: This separates people who get consistently good output from people who think the tool is inconsistent. The key is a specific, adversarial persona — "skeptical senior editor" works. "Make it better" doesn't. Note: the critique loop improves structure and clarity. It doesn't catch factual errors. You still read the output.
Single critique turn
Now critique what you just wrote as if you were a skeptical senior editor. List the three weakest parts, the two most confusing sentences, and one structural problem. Then revise.
Three-pass version (one prompt, all three steps)
Perform three passes.

Pass 1 — Draft: produce a first draft.

Pass 2 — Critique: switch into the persona of a skeptical senior reviewer; list exactly three weaknesses, most to least important.

Pass 3 — Revise: rewrite addressing every weakness. End with a "Changes" section, one line per change.
Persona by use case: Writing → "skeptical senior editor at [publication]" · Strategy → "adversarial peer reviewer who thinks this will fail" · Content → "a student who has never heard of this topic"

Try It
Take one thing you're about to write today — a Slack message, an email, a document, anything.
  • 1Write out who's actually going to read this — specifically.
  • 2Ask for five distinctly different versions before you pick one.
  • 3Run one critique loop on the version you choose before you send it.
